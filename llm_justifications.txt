SQL

SQL queries are generally structured and somewhat readable due to consistent clause ordering (SELECT, FROM, WHERE, etc.), which helps convey intent. However, readability drops significantly for hierarchical queries (Q3–Q7), where recursive constructs (WITH RECURSIVE, path tracking) introduce substantial cognitive overhead and obscure the high-level goal. As a first-time reader, the need to mentally simulate recursion and joins makes complex queries difficult to parse.

HierQL

HierQL is consistently the most readable across all queries because it expresses hierarchical intent in concise, almost natural-language-like constructs (e.g., “DISEASES UNDER,” “ANCESTORS,” “DEPTH”). The syntax minimizes boilerplate and makes relationships explicit without requiring the reader to infer traversal logic. Even complex hierarchical queries remain straightforward, with very low mental overhead.

Datalog

Datalog is difficult to read due to its rule-based, declarative style that requires understanding multiple intermediate predicates and recursion spread across definitions. The intent is often fragmented across several lines, making it hard to immediately grasp the final goal. While simple aggregations (Q2) are somewhat clearer, most queries require significant effort to mentally assemble the logic.

Cypher

Cypher is relatively readable because its pattern-matching syntax visually represents relationships as graph structures, which helps convey traversal intuitively. Variable-length path expressions (e.g., *0..3) make hierarchical queries especially concise and clear. However, some symbolic elements and directionality may still require minor interpretation, preventing it from being perfectly transparent.

SPARQL

SPARQL has moderate readability but suffers from verbosity and less intuitive syntax (e.g., prefixes, triple patterns, semicolon chaining). While hierarchical traversal using operators like * or + is compact, the overall structure requires careful attention to variable bindings and pattern grouping. More complex queries (especially Q7) become harder to follow due to indirect constructs like OPTIONAL and VALUES.

SQL performs adequately on flat queries (Q1–Q2), where English-like keywords (SELECT, FROM, WHERE, ORDER BY) map fairly naturally onto the intent, earning a 3. However, as soon as hierarchy is involved (Q3–Q7), the language requires a WITH RECURSIVE construct that forces the reader to mentally simulate a fixpoint computation, track an auxiliary path array to prevent cycles, and then rejoin the result — none of which is self-evident without deep prior knowledge. The intent of "find all subtypes" or "find ancestors" is completely hidden behind low-level recursion mechanics, making these queries nearly impenetrable to a newcomer and warranting a 1.
HierQL consistently earns the highest ratings across all seven queries. For flat queries it closely mirrors natural English with FIND / FROM / JOIN / WHERE, which is marginally clearer than SQL's SELECT. Its decisive advantage appears in Q3–Q7, where built-in constructs like DISEASES UNDER "...", DISEASES ANCESTORS "...", and DISEASES DEPTH "..." 3 encode the traversal intent in a single, self-describing phrase — no recursion scaffolding, no depth counters, no cycle guards. A reader unfamiliar with any of the languages can immediately understand what these clauses request, which is the core criterion being evaluated here.
Datalog is consistently the second-lowest scorer across all queries. The rule-based head :- body notation requires understanding that comma means logical AND, that :- means "is derived from," and that positional arguments like variant(vid, gid, rsid, _, _, _, _) carry implicit schema knowledge the reader must supply. Recursive queries (Q3–Q7) require tracing through multiple inter-dependent rule definitions before the overall intent becomes clear. That said, the logic is at least decomposed into named intermediate predicates (e.g., descendant, ancestor), which provides some structural transparency, preventing a rating of 1.
Cypher is the strongest language for hierarchical queries after HierQL, earning 4s on Q3, Q4, Q6, and Q7. The ASCII-art arrow notation (<-[:REL]->) visualizes relationships in a way that can feel intuitive even to newcomers. Flat queries (Q1–Q2) score slightly lower than HierQL because the direction of relationship arrows requires the reader to understand graph orientation, adding modest cognitive overhead.
SPARQL scores in the middle for simple queries (Q1–Q2) but degrades noticeably for hierarchical ones. Language Justifications
SQL
While simple filtering is clear, SQL becomes nearly illegible when handling hierarchies. The WITH RECURSIVE syntax requires the reader to understand complex procedural logic (like tracking paths in an array to avoid cycles) rather than simply stating what they want to find. For a new user, the jump from a basic SELECT to the "wall of text" required for graph traversal is a massive cognitive hurdle.

HierQL
This language is the most readable because its keywords (e.g., FIND, DISEASES UNDER, DEPTH) mirror the way a human would phrase the request. It successfully hides the technical "how" of the database behind a declarative "what," making even complex 3-hop or recursive searches immediately understandable to a layperson. The use of full words over symbols keeps the mental overhead minimal.

Datalog
Datalog feels like a puzzle that requires solving rather than a query to be read. The heavy reliance on positional arguments (the underscores _ in variant(vid, gid, rsid, _, _, _, _)) is a major barrier; a reader cannot know what those fields represent without external documentation. The logical "if-then" structure is elegant for computers but forces the human reader to manually stitch together disparate rules to see the big picture.

Cypher
Cypher is highly intuitive due to its "ASCII-art" style, where arrows (->) visually represent the relationships between data points. Using [:IS_A*0..] is a clever, concise way to denote "any distance," which is much easier to parse than SQL’s recursion. It strikes a great balance between being concise and being visual, though it uses more symbols than HierQL.

SPARQL
The readability of SPARQL suffers from significant visual noise caused by prefixes (bio:), question marks for variables, and the specific punctuation required for triple patterns. While it handles basic hierarchy well with property paths (isA*), the syntax for limited depth (like in Q7) is extremely clunky, requiring nested OPTIONAL blocks that are very difficult to follow.

Datalog
All queries score poorly because Datalog’s logic‑programming style is opaque to a novice. The :- separation of head and body, anonymous variables (_), and predicate‑based joins require prior exposure to declarative logic. Recursive definitions (e.g., descendant(child, root) :- disease_edge(...), descendant(parent, root)) are compact but cryptic. Aggregation syntax (count : count vid) is non‑standard and confusing. Cypher

Overall, Cypher performs best on path‑traversal queries (Q4, Q7) where the pattern directly mirrors the graph structure.

SPARQL
Triple patterns with ?variables are fairly clear for basic queries (Q1, Q2), and property paths (bio:isA*, bio:isA+) make hierarchical queries readable. However, the required PREFIX, curly braces, and dot notation add syntactic noise. The most serious drawback appears in Q7: expressing a depth limit (≤3 hops) forces an awkward workaround using OPTIONAL and VALUES, which is completely non‑obvious to a novice.
